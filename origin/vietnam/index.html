Terroir

Why Vietnamese soil produces
exceptional cacao

The Central Highlands' unique combination of volcanic geology, altitude, and microclimate creates conditions that concentrate flavour and polyphenol compounds in ways lowland origins cannot match.

🌋
Volcanic Basalt Soil

The red basalt soils of Đắk Lắk and Lâm Đồng are mineral-rich and exceptionally well-drained. High iron, potassium, and magnesium content drives complex flavour precursor development in the bean cotyledon.

🌡️
Diurnal Temperature Variation

Altitude-driven day-to-night temperature swings of 12–15°C slow bean development and concentrate polyphenols. The same mechanism that produces complexity in high-altitude coffee applies equally to cacao.

💧
Distinct Wet & Dry Seasons

Vietnam's defined monsoon cycle creates natural crop timing — a concentrated harvest window that allows farmers to process beans at peak ripeness rather than staggering harvests across an extended season.

🌳
Agroforestry Systems

Vietnamese cacao is traditionally intercropped with fruit trees, pepper, and hardwoods. This multi-canopy system provides 30–50% shade cover — mimicking the cacao tree's natural rainforest understory habitat and reducing disease pressure.

🦠
Native Microbial Diversity

Vietnam's fermentation microflora — the specific yeast and bacterial populations that colonise fermentation boxes — are distinct from West African or South American origins, producing characteristic fruity-acidic flavour profiles unique to Vietnamese terroir.

🤲
Smallholder Craft Scale

Most Vietnamese cacao farms are under 2 hectares. This small scale forces attentive hand-harvesting and individual pod selection — practices that are economically impossible in large-scale commodity plantations.

Our Sourcing Regions

Two distinct origins,
one supply chain

kakao.guru sources from two complementary growing regions in Vietnam's Central Highlands, each contributing distinct flavour characteristics to our ceremonial cacao.

Central Highlands · 500–800m
Đắk Lắk Province

Vietnam's largest cacao-producing province. Red basalt soils, consistent humidity, and well-established smallholder cooperatives with 15+ years of fermentation experience. Produces cacao with pronounced fruit acidity, clean fermentation character, and deep mineral finish. Our primary sourcing region — the home of kakao.guru's signature profile.

Mekong Delta · Sea Level
Bến Tre Province

Vietnam's original cacao-growing region, introduced in the 1980s. The Mekong Delta's alluvial soils produce cacao with a softer, rounder flavour profile — less acidity, more chocolate-forward, with distinctive coconut and tropical fruit notes from intercropping with palm. A complementary counterpoint to the Highland's intensity.

Post-Harvest Science

The fermentation craft that defines our cacao

Fermentation is not a passive process — it is the single greatest determinant of cacao quality. Our partner farmers in Đắk Lắk follow precision post-harvest protocols developed over decades of craft refinement.

01
Selective Pod Harvesting

Pods are hand-harvested at precise colour and sound indicators of ripeness. Unripe or overripe pods are excluded — both compromise fermentation chemistry and final flavour. Harvest windows are typically 2–3 days per tree.

02
Box Fermentation · 6–7 Days

Beans and pulp are loaded into tiered wooden fermentation boxes. A microbial succession — yeasts, then lactic acid bacteria, then acetic acid bacteria — transforms both pulp and bean chemistry over 6–7 days. Turning every 48 hours manages the aerobic/anaerobic gradient.

03
Sun Drying · 7–10 Days

Fermented beans are spread on raised wooden drying beds under full sun. Slow drying (7–10 days) allows continued flavour development and gradual acetic acid volatilisation — preventing the vinegary off-notes of fast mechanical drying.

04
Grading & Quality Control

Cut tests (slicing beans to assess fermentation index) are performed on each batch. Only beans with full fermentation — brown-to-purple cross-section, clean aroma, no slaty or mouldy defects — are accepted for ceremonial grade processing.
